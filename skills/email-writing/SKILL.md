# Email Writing Skill

## Purpose

Transform vague email intentions into polished, context-appropriate communication that achieves the sender's goal. This skill adapts tone, formality, length, and psychological framing based on purpose and recipient, then delivers multiple draft options so you choose what feels right.

---

## Input Requirements

The AI should gather (through conversation or structured form):

### Email Fundamentals

- **Purpose of email**: What do you want to happen? (inform, request, propose, decline, thank, apologize, announce, etc.)
- **Recipient(s)**: Who are you writing to? (title, relationship, how well do you know them)
- **Recipient's context**: What do they care about? What's their situation? (busy executive, peer collaborator, new contact, etc.)
- **Your relationship to recipient**: First time reaching out? Ongoing? Hierarchical? Peer?

### Tone & Content Preferences

- **Desired tone**: Formal, professional-warm, casual, enthusiastic, earnest, urgent, etc.
- **Length preference**: Brief (3 sentences), medium (paragraph), detailed (multiple paragraphs)
- **Key message**: What's the 1-2 things they absolutely need to know?
- **Call to action**: What do you want them to do? (reply, attend, approve, forward, etc.)
- **Deadline or urgency**: When do you need a response?

### Optional Enhancements

- **Specific details to include**: Names, dates, numbers, achievements
- **Tone of voice examples**: "Write it like you're texting a friend" or "Write it like a McKinsey consultant"
- **Things to avoid**: Topics/language that won't land well with this recipient
- **Previous context**: If replying, relevant background from prior emails

## Processing Steps

### Step 1: Identify the Email Context

Map the email to one of 10+ context types:

- **Leadership/Executive Update** — upward communication, structured format
- **Course/Workshop Announcement** — engaging, enthusiasm, benefit-focused
- **Bio/Abstract/Speaking Response** — professional, credentialed, compelling
- **Networking/Outreach** — warm, value-first, genuine interest
- **Customer/Client Support** — empathetic, solution-focused, clear next steps
- **Internal Team Update** — collaborative, context-setting, inclusive
- **Apology/Difficult Conversation** — sincere, accountable, forward-looking
- **Sales/Partnership Proposal** — compelling, credibility, mutual benefit
- **Decline/Difficult No** — kind, clear, alternative options
- **Thank You/Recognition** — specific, genuine, timely
- **Meeting Request/Scheduling** — clear value, flexible, respect their time

### Step 2: Analyze Recipient Psychology

- What's their current pressure/priority?
- How do they prefer to receive information? (bullets, narrative, data, story)
- What matters to them in this domain?
- What would make them say "yes"?
- What objections might they have?

### Step 3: Determine Tone & Formality Level

- Map purpose + recipient + relationship to appropriate tone
- Formality scale: Very Formal | Professional | Warm-Professional | Casual | Very Casual
- Emotional tone: Urgent | Grateful | Earnest | Enthusiastic | Neutral | Empathetic

### Step 4: Structure the Email

- **Subject line**: Specific, benefit-forward, sense of urgency if needed
- **Greeting**: Appropriate formality (Dear [Name] | Hi [Name] | Hey [Name])
- **Hook/Opening**: Why they should care (in first 1-2 sentences)
- **Body**: Key message, evidence, context (if needed)
- **Call to action**: Crystal clear what you want them to do
- **Closing**: Appropriate sign-off for tone (Sincerely | Best | Thanks | Cheers)

### Step 5: Draft Multiple Versions

Generate 2-3 alternatives:

- **Primary draft**: Your recommended version (best balance of tone/effectiveness)
- **Alternate A**: Slightly more formal version (if they might find primary too casual)
- **Alternate B**: Slightly warmer/more casual version (if primary feels stiff)

### Step 6: Quality Check

- Does it achieve the stated purpose?
- Is the tone consistent throughout?
- Is the CTA crystal clear?
- Did you avoid common mistakes for this context?
- Would the recipient feel respected/understood?

## Email Contexts (Detailed)

### 1. Leadership/Executive Update

**When:** Reporting to boss, CEO, board, senior stakeholders  
**Tone:** Formal, concise, results-oriented  
**Rules:**

- Assume they're busy; lead with the headline
- Include metrics/results before narrative
- Structure with clear sections (Status | Wins | Challenges | Next Steps)
- Be honest about problems; show you have solutions
- Respect their time (target: 3-4 paragraphs max)

**Opening:** "I wanted to share quick progress on [Project]. We're on track for [Goal]."  
**Closing:** "Please let me know if you'd like to discuss further."

---

### 2. Course/Workshop Announcement

**When:** Announcing a new learning opportunity  
**Tone:** Enthusiastic, benefit-focused, accessible  
**Rules:**

- Lead with transformation/benefit, not features
- Make it feel exclusive/timely
- Include clear how/when/where
- Reduce friction to saying yes
- Show who should attend

**Opening:** "We're launching something we think you'll love..."  
**Closing:** "Can't wait to see you there!"

---

### 3. Bio/Abstract/Speaking Response

**When:** Responding to request for credentials, speaker bio, talk abstract  
**Tone:** Professional, credible, compelling  
**Rules:**

- Bio: 3rd person, achievements, relevance to their context
- Abstract: Hook first, structure clear, benefit obvious
- Keep under 100 words for bio, 150 for abstract
- Show why this matters to their audience
- Include unique angle/perspective

**Structure:**

```
Bio: [Name] is a [title/expertise]. She has [relevant achievements].
At [company/context], she [key accomplishment]. Bio ends with
professional credential or speaking note.

Abstract: [Hook: why this matters]. This talk explores [topic].
You'll learn [3 takeaways]. Perfect for [audience].
```

---

### 4. Networking/Outreach

**When:** Cold outreach, reconnecting, collaboration inquiry  
**Tone:** Warm, genuine, value-first  
**Rules:**

- Personalize—show you know them
- Lead with how you can help THEM (not what you want)
- Be specific (not "let's connect," but "let's talk about X")
- Respect their time (suggest specific format)
- Make it easy to say yes

**Opening:** "I've admired your work on [specific thing]. I wanted to reach out because..."  
**Closing:** "I'd love to connect if you're open to it. How's [timing/format] for you?"

---

### 5. Customer/Client Support

**When:** Responding to question, complaint, or request  
**Tone:** Empathetic, solution-focused, reassuring  
**Rules:**

- Start with empathy, not excuse
- Acknowledge the problem specifically
- Show you understand the impact
- Provide clear solution/next steps
- Include timeline
- Offer to help further

**Opening:** "Thanks for reaching out. I understand how frustrating [issue] must be."  
**Closing:** "Let's get this resolved. Here's what I'll do..."

---

### 6. Internal Team Update

**When:** Updating colleagues, cross-team communication  
**Tone:** Collaborative, inclusive, context-setting  
**Rules:**

- Set context for why you're sharing
- Include team members' contributions
- Make space for input/questions
- Structure with headers for scannability
- Celebrate wins, acknowledge challenges

**Opening:** "Quick update on [Project] — wanted to keep everyone in the loop."  
**Closing:** "Questions or feedback? Let me know!"

---

### 7. Apology/Difficult Conversation

**When:** Apologizing, delivering bad news, difficult message  
**Tone:** Sincere, accountable, forward-looking  
**Rules:**

- Lead with acknowledgment (not excuse)
- Take responsibility specifically
- Show you understand impact
- Don't minimize or over-explain
- Offer concrete fix or path forward
- End on forward momentum

**Opening:** "I owe you an apology for [specific action/impact]."  
**Closing:** "Here's what I'm doing to make this right..."

---

### 8. Sales/Partnership Proposal

**When:** Pitching an idea, partnership, or service  
**Tone:** Compelling, credible, collaborative  
**Rules:**

- Lead with their problem/opportunity (not your solution)
- Show you understand their context
- Provide specific value/benefit
- Include proof points or credibility
- Make ask crystal clear (meeting, pilot, contract)
- Reduce perceived risk

**Opening:** "I've been thinking about your challenge with [X]. I think we might have something valuable."  
**Closing:** "I'd love to explore this with you. What would a next step look like?"

---

### 9. Decline/Difficult No

**When:** Saying no to request, opportunity, or invitation  
**Tone:** Kind, clear, respectful  
**Rules:**

- Give clear answer upfront (no hemming)
- Acknowledge why the ask is valuable
- Explain your constraint briefly (don't over-explain)
- Offer alternative if authentic
- Leave door open for future

**Opening:** "I appreciate you thinking of me. I need to be honest..."  
**Closing:** "Hope you find the right fit. Happy to refer if that helps."

---

### 10. Thank You/Recognition

**When:** Expressing gratitude, recognizing contribution  
**Tone:** Genuine, specific, warm  
**Rules:**

- Be specific about what they did
- Show impact of their action
- Make it personal, not generic
- Keep it brief and sincere
- Timing matters (send soon after)

**Opening:** "I wanted to thank you for [specific action]."  
**Closing:** "It made a real difference. Thank you."

---

### 11. Meeting Request/Scheduling

**When:** Asking for time, scheduling a conversation  
**Tone:** Respectful, clear, flexible  
**Rules:**

- Lead with clear value/purpose
- Respect their time (suggest specific length)
- Offer 2-3 specific time options
- Make it easy to say yes
- Include calendar link or clear next step

**Opening:** "I'd like to catch up on [topic]. Would you have 30 minutes in the next two weeks?"  
**Closing:** "Here are a few times that work for me [options]. What's best for you?"

## Expected Outputs

The skill should generate all of these outputs:

### 1. **Primary Email Draft**

- Recommended version balancing tone + effectiveness
- Ready to send (but you review first)
- Includes subject line + full body + sign-off

### 2. **Alternative Drafts (2)**

- **More Formal Version** — if primary feels too casual
- **More Warm/Casual Version** — if primary feels stiff
- Each with different subject line option

### 3. **Tone Analysis Card**

- Formality level chosen (Very Formal → Very Casual)
- Emotional tone (Urgent, Grateful, Earnest, etc.)
- Why these choices for this recipient/context

### 4. **Subject Line Options (3)**

- Primary recommendation
- Alternative for urgency
- Alternative for benefit/curiosity

### 5. **Recipient Psychology Brief**

- What they likely care about in this context
- What objections they might have
- What would make them say yes
- How to frame for maximum impact

### 6. **Editing Checklist**

- 10-15 specific quality checks
- Common mistakes for this context type
- Things to verify before sending

### 7. **Success Metrics**

- How will you know if this email worked?
- What response indicates success?
- What's the ideal timeline?

## Notes for AI Implementation

- **Personalization is key**: Reference specific details, past conversations, mutual connections
- **Recipient psychology**: Always ask "what does this person care about?" before drafting
- **Tone consistency**: Email tone should match relationship level + purpose, not just your natural voice
- **CTA clarity**: If there's no call to action, the email likely won't get the response you want
- **Alternative versions**: Generating 2-3 versions gives user agency to choose what feels right
- **Honesty about difficulty**: If declining something, acknowledge that first before explaining
- **Structure matters**: Executives scan bullets; friends read narrative; choose format for recipient
- **Proof points**: In sales/partnership emails, credibility matters—include specific wins if possible
- **Timing**: Suggest when they should send (morning vs. end of day, which day of week, etc.)
